# Emergency Response and Highway Safety

The speed and quality of emergency response after a highway accident is a critical determinant of whether injuries become fatalities. Brazil's emergency response infrastructure varies dramatically between urban and rural areas.

## SAMU — Serviço de Atendimento Móvel de Urgência

SAMU (Mobile Emergency Care Service) is Brazil's primary pre-hospital emergency medical service, analogous to ambulance services in other countries. SAMU operates through regional dispatch centers and is activated via the national emergency number 192. Coverage has expanded significantly since the program's creation in 2003, but gaps remain:

- **Urban areas:** SAMU coverage is generally reliable in cities with populations above 100,000. Average response time in urban areas is approximately 15 minutes from dispatch to arrival.
- **Smaller cities and rural areas:** SAMU bases are more sparse, and response times can exceed 30-60 minutes. Some municipalities lack dedicated SAMU bases entirely and rely on neighboring cities.
- **Remote highway stretches:** In rural segments far from population centers, response times can exceed one hour. This is particularly problematic on highways crossing sparsely populated areas such as stretches of BR-153 in Goiás, BR-116 in Bahia, and BR-163 in Mato Grosso.

## The Golden Hour

The concept of the "golden hour" in trauma medicine holds that critically injured patients have significantly better survival outcomes if they receive definitive medical care within 60 minutes of injury. On federal highways, this window is frequently missed in rural areas due to:

- Long response times for emergency services to reach the accident site
- Extended transport times from the accident site to an appropriate trauma center
- Limited availability of air ambulance (helicopter) services outside major metropolitan regions

The difference in fatality rates between urban and rural highway segments is partially explained by this response time gap. An accident with similar mechanism and severity may be survivable in an urban segment near a major hospital but fatal in a remote rural stretch where advanced care is hours away.

## PRF Response Protocols

The Federal Highway Police (PRF) is often the first responder to arrive at highway accident scenes. PRF officers are trained in basic first aid and scene management, including:

- Scene safety and traffic control to prevent secondary accidents
- Basic life support and hemorrhage control until SAMU arrives
- Accident documentation and cause investigation
- Coordination with SAMU, fire departments (Corpo de Bombeiros), and other agencies

PRF patrol vehicles carry basic medical supplies, but officers are not paramedics. Their primary role at accident scenes is stabilization and coordination rather than advanced medical intervention.

## Trauma Center Distribution

Brazil's trauma care infrastructure is concentrated in state capitals and larger cities. Level I trauma centers (capable of handling the most severe injuries) are primarily located in cities like São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, Belo Horizonte, Curitiba, Porto Alegre, Salvador, and Recife. This concentration means that seriously injured patients on rural highway stretches may need to be transported long distances — or airlifted — to reach appropriate care facilities.

The uneven distribution of trauma centers creates a geographic inequality in survival outcomes. A severe accident on the BR-101 near Florianópolis has access to multiple trauma facilities within 30 minutes, while a similar accident on the BR-116 in rural Bahia may be hours from the nearest capable hospital. This infrastructure gap is one of the most significant factors explaining regional differences in highway fatality rates across Brazil.
