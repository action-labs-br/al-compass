# Fatality Statistics — Brazilian Federal Highways

Brazil has made measurable progress in reducing highway fatalities over the past decade, though the absolute numbers remain high by international standards.

## National Trends

Federal highway fatalities have shown a declining trend over the past decade:

- **2015:** approximately 8,000 deaths on federal highways
- **2017:** approximately 6,800 deaths
- **2019:** approximately 5,900 deaths
- **2021:** approximately 5,400 deaths (partially influenced by reduced traffic during the pandemic)
- **2023:** approximately 5,600 deaths

The decline is attributed to a combination of factors: increased enforcement (radar cameras, breathalyzer checkpoints), road duplication projects converting single-carriageway to dual-carriageway, improved vehicle safety standards, and public awareness campaigns. However, the post-pandemic period showed a slight uptick as traffic volumes recovered faster than safety infrastructure improvements.

## State Comparison

Fatality counts vary significantly by state, influenced by population, highway network extent, and traffic volume:

- **Minas Gerais (MG):** Consistently the state with the highest absolute number of federal highway fatalities, reflecting its large territory, extensive highway network (including BR-381, BR-040, and BR-116 segments), and mountainous terrain.
- **Paraná (PR):** Second highest, with dangerous stretches of BR-376, BR-277, and BR-116 contributing to high fatality counts.
- **Santa Catarina (SC):** High fatality rates particularly on the BR-101 coastal stretch and BR-116 through the Serra Catarinense.
- **Bahia (BA):** Large territory and extensive highway network in the northeast, with BR-101 and BR-116 segments contributing significantly.
- **São Paulo (SP):** Despite having the largest population and traffic volume, a significant portion of São Paulo's highway network is state-managed (concessioned toll roads), so federal highway fatalities underrepresent the state's total.

## Rates per Population

When adjusted per 100,000 inhabitants, the ranking shifts. States like Tocantins, Mato Grosso do Sul, and Goiás show higher per-capita federal highway fatality rates due to extensive rural highway networks with low population density but significant freight traffic. Southern states (PR, SC, RS) also show elevated per-capita rates.

## International Comparison

Brazil's overall traffic fatality rate (including all road types) is approximately 15-18 deaths per 100,000 inhabitants, significantly higher than European countries (4-6 per 100,000) and the United States (~12 per 100,000). Federal highways account for roughly a quarter of all traffic deaths in Brazil, despite carrying a smaller share of total vehicle-km compared to state and municipal roads.

## Fatality vs. Injury Trends

While fatalities have declined, the total number of accidents has not decreased proportionally. This suggests that safety improvements (vehicle standards, road infrastructure, emergency response) are saving lives in accidents that would previously have been fatal, rather than preventing accidents from occurring in the first place. The ratio of fatalities to total accidents has improved from approximately 6% in 2015 to approximately 4.5% in 2023.
