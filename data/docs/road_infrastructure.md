# Road Infrastructure and Safety

The physical characteristics of Brazilian federal highways play a critical role in accident frequency and severity. Road type, geometry, speed limits, and supporting infrastructure all influence risk.

## Road Types

Federal highways are classified into three main categories based on their carriageway configuration:

- **Simples (Single carriageway):** A single roadway shared by traffic in both directions, typically with one lane per direction separated by painted center lines. This is the **most dangerous road type**, as it allows head-on collisions between vehicles traveling in opposite directions. The majority of fatal overtaking accidents occur on single-carriageway stretches. A large portion of the federal highway network remains single-carriageway, particularly in less-developed regions.
- **Dupla (Dual carriageway):** Two separate roadways divided by a physical barrier or wide median. Each carriageway carries traffic in one direction with one or more lanes. The physical separation eliminates head-on collisions between opposing traffic, significantly reducing fatality rates. Duplication projects have been a major federal investment priority, with measurable safety improvements documented after completion.
- **Múltipla (Multiple lanes):** Highways with three or more lanes in each direction, typically found near major metropolitan areas. These have the lowest fatality rates per vehicle-km but can still experience severe accidents due to high traffic volume and speed.

## Speed Limits

Speed limits on federal highways vary by road type and land use context:

- **Urban segments:** 60 km/h (some stretches may be 40-50 km/h in school zones or high-pedestrian areas)
- **Rural single-carriageway:** 80 km/h for cars, 60 km/h for trucks and buses
- **Rural dual-carriageway:** 100-110 km/h for cars, 80-90 km/h for trucks and buses

Speed incompatible with conditions remains one of the leading causes of fatal accidents, even when drivers are within the posted limit. Wet roads, fog, nighttime conditions, and heavy traffic all require speeds well below the posted maximum for safe operation.

## Safety Infrastructure

Key safety infrastructure elements that affect accident outcomes include:

- **Guardrails and barriers:** Metal guardrails (defensas metálicas) on curves, bridges, and elevated stretches prevent vehicles from leaving the roadway. Coverage remains incomplete on many rural stretches.
- **Lighting:** Adequate lighting significantly reduces nighttime accident severity. Urban segments are generally well-lit, but rural stretches are largely unlit, making nighttime driving inherently more dangerous.
- **Signage:** Warning signs for curves, intersections, and speed changes. Electronic variable message signs on newer stretches provide real-time warnings about conditions ahead.
- **Rumble strips:** Installed on shoulders and center lines of some highways to alert drowsy or distracted drivers. Coverage has expanded in recent years but remains far from universal.

## Urban vs. Rural

Accidents in rural areas have significantly higher fatality rates than urban accidents. Rural stretches feature higher speeds, longer emergency response times, greater distance to trauma centers, and less safety infrastructure. Urban segments have more frequent but less severe accidents, primarily low-speed rear-end collisions and sideswipes in congested traffic. The transition zones between rural and urban speed limits are particularly dangerous, as drivers often fail to reduce speed adequately when entering lower-speed zones.
