# Vehicle Types and Fatality Risk

The type of vehicle involved in an accident is a strong predictor of injury severity and fatality risk. Brazilian federal highways carry a diverse mix of vehicle types, each with distinct risk profiles.

## Motorcycles

Motorcycles represent one of the most vulnerable vehicle categories on federal highways. Despite accounting for a smaller share of highway traffic compared to cars, **motorcyclists account for approximately 25% of all highway fatalities**. The lack of physical protection means that even moderate-speed collisions frequently result in serious injuries or death. Common accident types involving motorcycles include collisions with larger vehicles, single-vehicle falls (queda de motocicleta), and sideswipes during lane filtering. Motorcycle accidents are more common in the northeast and north regions, where motorcycles are a primary mode of transportation even on federal highways.

## Passenger Cars (Automóvel)

Passenger cars are involved in the largest absolute number of accidents on federal highways. However, per-incident fatality rates are lower than for motorcycles or pedestrians due to modern safety features (airbags, crumple zones, seatbelts). The most dangerous accident types for car occupants are head-on collisions (colisão frontal) on single-carriageway roads and rollovers (capotamento) at high speeds. Rear-end collisions (colisão traseira) are the most frequent type but usually have lower severity.

## Trucks and Commercial Vehicles

Heavy trucks (caminhão) and articulated vehicles (carreta) present a dual risk profile. Due to their mass, collisions involving trucks and smaller vehicles result in **significantly higher fatality rates for the occupants of the smaller vehicle**. The mass differential means that a truck-car collision is far more likely to be fatal than a car-car collision at the same speed. Truck drivers themselves face lower per-incident fatality risk due to the cab's elevated position and vehicle mass, but long-haul driver fatigue is a major contributing factor to truck-involved accidents. Brake failure on descents is another significant cause, particularly on mountainous highways like the BR-116 and BR-381.

## Pedestrians

Pedestrians involved in highway accidents face the **highest per-occurrence fatality rate** of any category. Pedestrian accidents on federal highways typically occur in segments passing through urban areas or small towns, where people cross the highway on foot. The high speeds of highway traffic make pedestrian collisions extremely dangerous. Inadequate crosswalks, poor lighting, and the absence of pedestrian overpasses in many urban-segment stretches contribute to the problem.

## Bicycles

Bicycle accidents on federal highways are concentrated in urban segments and peri-urban areas where cyclists share the road with high-speed traffic. The absence of dedicated cycling infrastructure on most federal highways forces cyclists onto the shoulder or main lanes. Fatality rates for cyclists are high, though the absolute numbers are lower than for motorcycles and pedestrians due to fewer cyclists using federal highways for long-distance travel.

## Key Insight

The interaction between vehicle types is often more important than the vehicle type alone. Accidents involving mixed vehicle types — particularly heavy vehicles colliding with motorcycles, bicycles, or pedestrians — produce the most severe outcomes. Single-type collisions (car-car, truck-truck) tend to have more moderate outcomes when both vehicles provide similar levels of occupant protection.
